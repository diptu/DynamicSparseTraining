# 10-Week Outcome-Based Plan — "Where Should the Bits Go?"

**Definition of done (Week 10):** arXiv preprint live + `diptu.github.io` updated with the paper & repo + 3 cold emails sent.
**Operating rule:** each week is defined by a single *outcome that either exists or does not by Sunday night* — not by hours spent. If the outcome isn't real, cut scope; never extend the timeline.
**Why 10 and not 8:** the two extra weeks are deliberate buffer, both on the risky side of the project — one on the proof (Weeks 2–3), one separating first-draft from revision (Weeks 8–9). Everything else is engineering and writing, which is low-risk.

## Standing rules

1. **Week 3 is the only gate.** Everything after it is engineering and writing you already know you can do. If the math fails twice, simplify the claim — do not spend a later week stuck on a proof.
2. **Write while you read.** Reading notes go straight into `related.tex`, never into a separate file to be transcribed later.
3. **Pre-register predictions (Week 5).** Committing the predicted allocations *before* the comparison runs is nearly free and makes the result far more convincing.
4. **Scope only shrinks — never grows.** Allowed cuts, in strict order: Qwen model → LongBench subsets → per-head granularity → ablations. **Never cut:** the lemma, the bound-vs-measured plot, the three-way allocation comparison.
5. **~10 focused hrs/week minimum.** Weeks 6–7 are the heavy-compute weeks — queue overnight runs. A 1B model on Colab Pro / a single consumer GPU is sufficient for everything here.
6. **Friday commit ritual.** Every Friday: push, update the README results table, and add a one-line progress note to `notes/log.md`. Ten entries = a public record of consistent research output, which is itself application material.
7. **Buffer is not free time.** Weeks 2–3 and 8–9 exist to absorb the two known risks (the proof, the revision). If a risk doesn't materialize, pull the *next* week's outcome forward — bank the slack, don't spend it.
